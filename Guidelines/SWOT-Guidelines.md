# SWOT Analysis Guidelines

## Overview
SWOT analysis evaluates Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats for strategic planning. The toolkit converts plain text SWOT format into professional Draw.io quadrant diagrams.

## Basic Input Format
```
Strengths:
- Strong brand recognition
- Experienced team
- Innovative products

Weaknesses:
- Limited market presence
- High operational costs
- Dependency on key suppliers

Opportunities:
- Growing market demand
- New technology trends
- Strategic partnerships

Threats:
- Increasing competition
- Economic uncertainty
- Regulatory changes
```

## Best Practices

### Content Quality
- **Specific items**: Use concrete examples, not vague statements
- **Balanced analysis**: Include 3-5 items per quadrant
- **Actionable insights**: Focus on strategic implications
- **Evidence-based**: Support claims with data or examples

### Strategic Focus
- **Internal factors**: Strengths and weaknesses (controllable)
- **External factors**: Opportunities and threats (uncontrollable)
- **Current reality**: Present state assessment
- **Future orientation**: Forward-looking opportunities and threats

### Analysis Depth
- **Root causes**: Understand why factors exist
- **Impact assessment**: Evaluate significance and likelihood
- **Interconnections**: Note how factors relate to each other
- **Prioritization**: Rank items by importance and urgency

## Strategic Applications

### TOWS Matrix Integration
SWOT analysis often leads to TOWS matrix development:

**SO Strategies** (Strengths + Opportunities):
- Leverage strengths to exploit opportunities
- Use core competencies for market expansion

**WO Strategies** (Weaknesses + Opportunities):
- Address weaknesses to pursue opportunities
- Improve capabilities to capitalize on trends

**ST Strategies** (Strengths + Threats):
- Use strengths to minimize threats
- Leverage advantages against competitive pressures

**WT Strategies** (Weaknesses + Threats):
- Defensive strategies to minimize weaknesses and avoid threats
- Consider strategic withdrawal or divestment

### Action Planning
- **Prioritize actions**: Focus on high-impact, feasible initiatives
- **Set timelines**: Establish realistic implementation schedules
- **Assign responsibility**: Designate owners for each action
- **Monitor progress**: Track implementation and results

### Research Methods
- **Internal analysis**: Review performance data, employee feedback
- **Market research**: Customer surveys, competitor analysis
- **Industry reports**: Market trends, regulatory changes
- **Expert consultation**: Stakeholder interviews, expert opinions

### Validation Techniques
- **Cross-checking**: Verify factors with multiple sources
- **Peer review**: Get feedback from team members
- **Data validation**: Support claims with metrics and evidence
- **Scenario testing**: Test assumptions under different conditions

## Common Pitfalls

### Analysis Errors
- **Bias**: Personal opinions over objective analysis
- **Overemphasis**: Too many items diluting focus
- **Vagueness**: General statements without specifics
- **Static view**: Not considering changing conditions

### Strategic Mistakes
- **Incomplete analysis**: Missing key factors
- **Poor prioritization**: Treating all items equally
- **Lack of action**: Analysis without implementation
- **No follow-up**: Failing to monitor and adjust

## Troubleshooting

### Content Issues
- **Unbalanced quadrants**: Ensure similar depth in each area
- **Vague statements**: Make factors specific and measurable
- **Missing context**: Provide background for strategic factors
- **Overlapping items**: Clarify distinctions between factors

### Conversion Problems
- **Format errors**: Follow exact text structure requirements
- **Special characters**: Use plain text formatting
- **Long entries**: Break into concise bullet points
- **Encoding issues**: Save as UTF-8 plain text</content>
